# The Geography of Immigration Enforcement: Exploring Border Surveillance Patterns and IIRIRA 287(g) County Participation in Texas
## Project Overview
This project asks how the geographic expansion of immigration enforcement infrastructure (detention facilities, surveillance towers, and 287(g)-deputized agencies) has reshaped detention outcomes and legal access for non-citizen residents in Texas between 1994, when Customs and Border Protection (CBP) formally established its "Prevention Through Deterrence" doctrine, and January 2026. Federal immigration enforcement has evolved substantially since then: through the Obama-era Secure Communities (S-Comm) program, which systematized biometric data sharing by routing all fingerprints booked into local jails through Department of Homeland Security (DHS) databases, to present-day data-sharing partnerships among ICE, commercial data brokers, government databases, and local law enforcement.
Section 287(g) agreements under the IIRIRA (Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act) of 1996 have been central to this evolution. In the first thirteen years following IIRIRA's passage, only one Texas county had applied to partner with ICE under 287(g), Harris County (the City of Houston), which remained the sole Texas participant through 2012 before Sheriff Gonzalez terminated the agreement in 2017. The pace of adoption accelerated dramatically under Governor Greg Abbott's Operation Lone Star program, which began in 2021. More than 150 out of 254 counties had active local law enforcement partnerships with ICE under 287(g) prior to July 2025. In July 2025, the Texas Legislature passed State Senate Bill (SB 8) requiring all eligible Texas Counties to enter into a 287(g) agreement, i.e. 234 of 254 counties, by the end of December 2026.  This will effectively force local and state authorities to collaborate with ICE/CBP and grant local law enforcement to assume federal immigration enforcement powers through three types of programs: Warrant Service Officer (WSO) Model, Jail Enforcement Model (JEM), or Task Force Model (TFM).

## Related Literature 
Early research on 287(g) adoption, particularly the methods of analysis and hypothesis raised by Wong (2012),  remains relevant for understanding local cooperation with federal immigration authorities. However there is a significant gap in geospatial literature since the dramatic escalation of 287(g) participation and state-level enforcement infrastructure investment during the Operation Lone Star period (2021-2025). With only 13 courts serving 254 counties, the ratio of enforcement infrastructure to legal capacity is an additional dimension to be addressed in this work. Using OSMnx routing analysis, PostGIS spatial joins, and drive-time isochrone modeling, this project extends the geospatial literature on 287(g) into the present enforcement landscape.

## Research Questions
My work aims to complement findings readily available from the [Texas Data Immigration Portal](), powered by the Texas Immigration Law Council, using geospatial modeling techniques. To quantify the impacts of uneven distributions of legal support and highly surveilled border sectors/stations in areas with concentrations of non-citizen immigrants, hispanic speaking populations, and [] detainees [i.e. only 13 courts to serve 254 counties], I will:
a) Perform nearest-neighbor distance calculations to measure geographic accessibility to immigration courts from detention facilities, including service area ratios (judges per county, detainees/case filings per court, deportation/removal orders per judge).
b) Test whether immigration enforcement density (number of detention beds, designated immigration officer staffing levels, and border patrol personnel deployment by sector) has expanded spatially over time by creating choropleth maps and time-series visualizations tracking surveillance infrastructure patterns (RVSS and AST tower installation dates) alongside 287(g) agreement signing dates.
c) Model the relationship between DPS, CBP, and DHS contract spending data and county-level variables (287(g) participation status, political voting patterns, unemployment rate disparities between non-citizens and citizens, and Spanish-speaking community percentages) to quantify the relative influence of partisan ideology, labor market conditions, and other demographic factors on shaping local law enforcement collaboration with ICE and federal investment patterns into surveillance technology deployments. 

## Data
* <ins>ICE Detention Facilities (Vera Institute / TRAC):</ins> Facility-level point data including type, AOR assignment, and coordinates (161 active facilities after hospital exclusion). Vera’s ICE Detention Trends [GitHub](https://github.com/vera-institute/ice-detention-trends/blob/main/metadata/facilities.csv) was the primary source, supplemented by Syracuse University's [Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse (TRAC)](https://tracreports.org/immigration/detentionstats/facilities.html) Immigration database to ensure completeness.
* <ins>ICE AOR Shapefiles ([UC Berkeley Deportation Data Project](https://deportationdata.org/data/processed/ice-offices.html)):</ins> County-level AOR polygons preprocessed and assigned to five Texas AORs — Dallas (128 counties), Houston (57), San Antonio (36), El Paso (18), Harlingen (15). Used to dissolve counties into AORs for all analysis.
* <ins>Immigration Court Data (DOJ EOIR):</ins> Court addresses and facility-to-court assignments scraped from the [EOIR Immigration Court Administrative Control List](https://www.justice.gov/eoir/immigration-court-administrative-control-list); judge counts from the EOIR Find a Court resource. Addresses were geocoded via geopy (ArcGIS geocoder), producing point geometries for 12 of 13 Texas courts. Fort Worth IAC lacked a geocodable address. I also acquired data on the number of judges from [this EOIR resource](https://www.justice.gov/eoir/find-immigration-court-and-access-internet-based-hearings#TX) to study how the geographic context of courts affect case burden and outcomes.
* <ins>Surveillance Towers (EFF, compiled by Dave Maass):</ins> 292 Texas towers — RVSS (173) and AST (109) — with vendor attribution (General Dynamics, Anduril). Dataset choice was informed by Chambers & Soto (2025), whose KS-test analysis of Integrated Fixed Tower deployments demonstrated that ASTs are disproportionately placed in terrain without road access. [The Electronic Frontiers Fund (EFF) dataset](https://www.google.com/maps/d/u/1/viewer?mid=1bxUGeOT6vVXu0jFQhDLxgktLFLVOKsI&ll=27.507870239756873%2C-98.42008585231306&z=7) extends that finding to all tower types at scale.
* <ins>287(g) Participating Agencies (ICE, as of February 2026):</ins> Agency-level agreement data including signing dates, agency type, and program model (WSO/JEM/TFM) for 178 agencies across 146 counties. Pivoted to binary model-type columns per agency to enable choropleth mapping and co-deployment correlation with tower locations.
* <ins>Surveillance Spending ([Texas Legislative Budget Board (LBB) Contracts Database](https://contracts.lbb.texas.gov/)):</ins> Contracts and planned spending data filtered 'Agency' to Texas Department of Public Safety (DPS) to search for and find Border Patrol spending.

## Methods
PostGIS Database Operations: Created a PostGIS database (tx_immigration_enforcement, EPSG:4269) and loaded all data using shp2pgsql and ogr2ogr. Spatial join operations via ST_Within produced derived tables: AOR-to-county polygon dissolves, AOR-level tower and court assignments, detention facility records, and judge detail tables. These tables served as inputs to all subsequent analyses and PostGIS summary queries (tower density per AOR, judge-to-facility ratio, 287(g) model distribution, and co-deployment correlation).
OSMnx Networks and Nearest-Node Snapping: Drive-network graphs were downloaded for the Harlingen and El Paso AORs using ox.graph_from_polygon() on the unary_union of each AOR’s county geometries, projected to EPSG:3857, and saved as .GraphML files. Immigration court and surveillance (RVSS and AST) tower data was snapped to the nearest OSM road node using ox.distance.nearest_nodes(). Distances between the actual tower coordinates and the snapped nearest node exceeding 1.5 km (1500m) were excluded from node_gdfs for quality assurance purposes. 
Routing Network Analysis: For each immigration court in an AOR, shortest paths to all detention facilities were computed using travel_times and `nx.single_source_dijkstra_path_length()` from the court’s nearest node. Actual route geometries for the five farthest facilities per court were retrieved with ox.routing.route_to_gdf() and visualized as route maps color-coded by travel time. Results were aggregated by AOR to produce court-level accessibility tables.
Computing Tower Drive-Time Isochrones: Isochrone polygons were generated at 5, 10, and 20-minute thresholds (300/600/1200 seconds) using nx.ego_graph() with weight='travel_time'. Each reachable subgraph was buffered (100 m) and unioned via shapely.ops.unary_union into a filled polygon by travel times. Areas were computed in EPSG:6579 (Texas Centric Lambert Conformal Conic), an equal-area projection appropriate for Texas, and rate-limit interruptions prevented completion of the San Antonio, Houston, and Dallas AORs street network downloads.

## Limitations and Next Steps
1) San Antonio, Houston, and Dallas AORs were not fully analyzed due to Overpass rate limits during the notebook run. These three AORs together contain 221 of 254 Texas counties and warrant equivalent analysis.
2) Off-road mobility of AST deployments is not captured by OSMnx drive-network graphs. A future extension could incorporate terrain raster data and off-road traversal costs.
3) Temporal analysis of 287(g) agreement signing dates (available in counties_287g.signed) against tower installation dates would enable a causal claim about whether local deputization preceded or followed tower deployment in each county.
4) The alternatives-to-detention framing of the original project proposal — treating ATD programs as surveillance extension rather than social service — would benefit from incorporating BI Inc. GPS monitoring data as a fourth spatial layer.
